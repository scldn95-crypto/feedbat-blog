Feedback meetings in agencies often follow a predictable pattern. Someone presents the work. The room goes quiet. A few people offer opinions. Someone senior says something that shifts the whole discussion. Thirty minutes pass and nothing definitive has been agreed. You leave with a vague sense of direction and a list of conflicting notes.

This is not inevitable. Feedback meetings can be focused, decisive, and fast, but only if they are structured properly before anyone enters the room.

## The Problem With Most Feedback Meetings

The root issue is that most feedback meetings try to do two things at once: share the work and review it simultaneously. People are forming opinions in real time while other people are already commenting. Nobody has had time to think. The feedback is reactive, surface-level, and inconsistent.

This is why async review before a synchronous meeting produces consistently better outcomes. When stakeholders have already seen the work, left their initial notes, and had time to think, the meeting becomes a decision-making session rather than a first impression. The quality of the conversation is higher, the discussion is more focused, and decisions get made.

## Structure the Meeting Before the Work Is Shared

If you are going to hold a feedback meeting, set the structure in advance. Tell attendees what the meeting is for: not to review the work from scratch, but to discuss and resolve the notes that have already been collected.

Send the work for async review 24-48 hours before the meeting. Ask each stakeholder to submit their notes in writing before they arrive. When you open the meeting, everyone is already informed and has already done the cognitive work of forming a view.

This changes the dynamic completely. Instead of watching and reacting, people arrive ready to discuss. The meeting is shorter, more focused, and more likely to end with clear decisions.

## Set an Agenda and Stick to It

A feedback meeting without an agenda becomes a conversation. A feedback meeting with an agenda becomes a decision-making process.

The agenda should cover the specific items that need resolution, not a general invitation to review everything again. Work through the collected feedback notes, surface any conflicts or ambiguities, and agree a clear direction on each one. Do not let the discussion wander into ideas that were not part of the original brief, note them as future considerations and move on.

The person running the meeting is responsible for keeping it on track. That usually means the account lead or project manager, not the creative presenting the work. When someone goes off-agenda, redirect: Good point, let us note that and come back to it after we have resolved the main points.

## Give Everyone Space But Not Equal Time

One of the quieter problems in feedback meetings is that the loudest voice wins. A senior stakeholder or a particularly confident team member ends up setting direction, and the other people in the room either agree or stay quiet. The result is feedback that reflects one person's opinion, not the room's.

Before the discussion opens up, ask each person for one thing they think is working well and one thing they think needs to change. This quick round ensures every voice is heard before anyone starts to influence the group. It often surfaces useful perspectives from people who would otherwise stay quiet.

After the round, you can open to discussion, but the initial views are already captured.

## Document Decisions in Real Time

The worst outcome of a feedback meeting is walking away with ambiguous notes about what was agreed. Use a shared document or project tool to record decisions as they are made. Not a summary written up afterwards, decisions captured in the moment, visible to everyone in the room.

When someone agrees on a change, write it down where everyone can see. When a note is deferred or rejected, note that too and note why. By the end of the meeting, the decision log is the briefing document for the next revision round.

FeedBat complements this process well. If your team does async feedback collection before the meeting, all the notes are already centralised and in context. The meeting becomes a layer on top of that, a place to resolve the things that could not be resolved through written notes alone. After the meeting, the final decisions go back into the feedback tool and the revision round begins from a clear, agreed brief. That is how feedback meetings should end: with everyone aligned and the project moving forward.
