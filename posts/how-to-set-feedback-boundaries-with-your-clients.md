You finish a project. You send it over. The client comes back with a wall of notes, a voice memo, three WhatsApp messages, and a PDF their colleague annotated. Sound familiar? This is what happens when feedback has no boundaries and it costs you time, energy, and often money.

Setting feedback boundaries is not about being difficult. It is about creating a professional process that helps both you and your clients get better results, faster.

## Why Feedback Without Boundaries Always Costs More

When there is no structure around feedback, clients default to whatever feels easiest for them. That usually means scattered notes across email, text, video calls, and comment threads. You end up spending hours piecing together what they actually want before you can do any real work.

Worse, open-ended feedback invites scope creep. Without a clear process, clients feel like each round of feedback is an opportunity to add new ideas, change direction, or rethink the brief. You end up doing more work than you agreed to, often without getting paid for it.

The solution is not to be rigid or unhelpful. It is to set clear expectations before the project starts so everyone knows how feedback works.

## Start With a Feedback Policy in Your Contract

The best place to establish boundaries is in your contract. Include a section that covers three things: how many rounds of revisions are included, what counts as a revision versus a new request, and how feedback should be submitted.

Keep it simple. Something like two rounds of consolidated feedback via the project portal is enough. The key word is consolidated. Every note from every stakeholder, in one place, at one time.

This does a few things. It stops you receiving drip-feed feedback over days. It forces clients to actually talk to each other before sending notes. And it gives you a clear reference point if someone tries to add extra rounds later.

If clients push back on this, frame it as a benefit for them. A structured process means faster turnarounds, fewer miscommunications, and a cleaner final result. That is genuinely true and most clients respond well to that framing.

## Create a Feedback Channel, Not a Feedback Free-for-All

Once you have your policy in writing, you need one place for all feedback to live. Not email. Not WhatsApp. Not a mix of both with a side of voice notes.

A dedicated feedback tool means everything is in context, alongside the actual work. Clients can leave comments directly on the relevant section. Stakeholders can see what others have already said, which reduces duplicate notes. You can respond, mark things as resolved, and keep a clear record of what was agreed.

This matters more than people realise. When feedback is scattered, it is easy for things to get missed or for clients to feel like you ignored their notes even if you did not. Centralising it removes that ambiguity.

## Be Explicit About What Happens When Boundaries Are Crossed

Setting boundaries only works if you follow through when they are tested. And they will be tested. A client will send a WhatsApp at 10pm. Someone will try to sneak in a fourth round of changes without asking. A stakeholder will email you directly, bypassing the agreed process.

When that happens, redirect clearly and without drama. Thanks for this, can you add it to the feedback portal so everything stays in one place? is all you need. You are not being awkward. You are protecting the process you both agreed to.

If extra rounds are needed, be ready to quote for them. A short, professional message such as Happy to incorporate this, it falls outside the two agreed rounds so I will send a quick change order is much easier to send if your contract already covers it. You are not making it up on the spot. You are just following what was agreed.

## The Difference Between Firmness and Inflexibility

There is an important distinction here. Setting feedback boundaries is not the same as refusing to help clients. You still want to be responsive, collaborative, and easy to work with.

The goal is to channel that collaboration into a process that works for everyone. Clients get clarity. You get consolidated, actionable feedback. The project moves forward without chaos.

When clients understand why the process exists, most of them appreciate it. It makes them feel like they are working with someone professional who takes their project seriously. That reputation is worth protecting.
