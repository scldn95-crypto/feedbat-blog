Most people think they give good feedback. Most people do not. This is not a character flaw, it is a skill, and it is rarely taught. We learn to give feedback on the job, often by absorbing the habits of whoever we are working with, good or bad.

The result is that the quality of feedback in creative projects varies enormously. Some clients give precise, useful notes that move a project forward. Others send vague impressions, contradictory comments, or a wall of thoughts that takes longer to interpret than it would to just redo the work. As a creative, you know which type you would rather be dealing with.

The good news is that great feedback is learnable. And understanding what it looks like in practice, whether you are giving it or receiving it, changes how well creative projects go.

## Great Feedback Is Specific

The most common failure in feedback is vagueness. It does not feel right. Can we make it pop more? Something is off. These notes describe an emotional reaction without pointing at anything actionable.

Specific feedback names the thing, describes the problem, and ideally suggests a direction: The intro paragraph is too long, it takes four sentences to get to the point. Can we get to the hook by sentence two? That is three layers of useful information: what, why, and where to go.

You do not need to have the solution. Noting the problem specifically is enough. The blue in the header feels too bright against the white background is specific even without a suggested fix. The creative knows what to look at and can bring their expertise to solving it.

## Great Feedback Comes at the Right Time

Timing is as important as content. Feedback delivered too late compresses timelines and forces rushed revisions. Feedback delivered in drips, one person today, another three days later, fragments your understanding of what is actually needed.

Good feedback is consolidated and timely. All stakeholders review at the same time, in the same place, and submit their notes as a single response within an agreed window. This sounds simple but it requires coordination that many organisations are not set up for.

When you give feedback, aim to do it as one complete review rather than a running commentary. Read or watch the whole thing first. Form a view. Then comment. You will give better notes and you will give fewer of them.

## Great Feedback Distinguishes Must-Fix From Nice-to-Have

Not all feedback is equal, but it is often presented as if it is. When a client sends ten notes, some of those will be essential changes and some will be personal preferences. When those two things are mixed together without any prioritisation, the creative has to guess which is which.

Good feedback is prioritised. It separates the things that need to change from the things that would be nice to change. A simple way to do this is to label notes as required or suggested. Required means the work cannot proceed without this change. Suggested means it would improve the piece but is not a blocker.

This prioritisation makes revision rounds faster and more focused. Creatives can address required changes first, then surface suggested changes for a conversation about whether they are worth the additional time.

## Great Feedback Is Tied to the Objective

The most useful feedback connects the note to the goal. Instead of I do not like this, the note is This does not work for our audience because. Instead of change the colour, it is We need to use our brand palette here to stay consistent with our other marketing materials.

Context makes feedback actionable in the right direction. Without it, creatives have to interpret your preferences and may solve the problem in a way that creates a different one.

Before you leave a note, ask yourself: why does this need to change? If you can answer that clearly, include the answer. If you cannot, the note might be a personal preference rather than a legitimate design issue, and that is worth questioning before you send it.

## The Environment Where Feedback Happens Matters

Even excellent feedback loses impact if it is delivered badly. Notes that arrive in a long email thread without context, or split across three messages over two days, are harder to work with than the same notes left directly on the relevant section of the work.

A centralised review environment changes the quality of feedback even before the notes are written. When reviewers can see and react to the actual work, their comments are more precise. When they can see what others have already said, they add to the conversation rather than duplicating it. And when everything is in one place, the creative can work through each note systematically rather than piecing together a picture from scattered sources.
