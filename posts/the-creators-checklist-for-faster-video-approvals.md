Video approvals are slow. Not because the work is bad, usually it is not, but because the process around the approval is unclear. The brand is not sure who needs to sign off. Feedback arrives from three different people through four different channels. And you are left waiting, watching your publishing window close.

Most creators just absorb this as the cost of working with brands. But a lot of the delay is structural, and it is fixable, especially on your side of the process. Here is a checklist to work through before you send anything for approval.

## Before You Share the Video

Define who is approving. Before you deliver the video, ask the brand to confirm one contact who is responsible for collecting internal feedback and giving final sign-off. You are not excluding other stakeholders, you are making sure there is one accountable person rather than a committee with no chair.

Set a review deadline. Tell the brand when you need feedback by. Do not leave the timeline open-ended. Please share notes by Thursday EOD so I can publish Friday is clear, professional, and gives them time to prepare without giving them unlimited time to delay.

Share via a review link, not an attachment. A download link or WeTransfer file puts the video somewhere that is difficult for teams to review together. A dedicated review link, where comments can be left directly on the video, makes the review process easier and faster for everyone involved.

## When Feedback Arrives

Read everything before you action anything. Before you start making changes, read through all the notes once. This lets you spot contradictions before they become your problem. If two stakeholders want opposite things, better to raise that before you have revised rather than after.

Separate creative notes from compliance notes. Brand feedback typically contains a mix of subjective preferences and hard requirements. Can we make it feel a bit warmer? is a creative preference. We need to add a disclaimer before the product mention is a compliance requirement. Address compliance first, then discuss creative direction. Never reverse that order.

Confirm your interpretation before revising. For complex or ambiguous notes, send a quick summary of what you plan to change before you action it. This catches misunderstandings early and prevents a second round for things that could have been aligned on immediately.

## Before the Final Delivery

Do a compliance check on the final version. Run through any platform-specific requirements, brand guidelines, and the specific compliance notes that were flagged. Deliver the final version with a brief note confirming which changes were made, so the approver can see their notes were acted on.

Keep a record of the approval. Once the final version is approved, get that confirmation in writing, even just a looks great, go ahead in the review tool or via email. If anything is questioned after publication, that record protects you.

Document what worked. After each project, note what made the review round smooth or complicated. Over time this gives you a picture of which brands have clear processes, which stakeholders are useful to involve early, and where you need to push harder on structure up front.
