There is a specific kind of project delay that agency people know well. The work is done. The team is ready to move. And nothing is happening because you are waiting for someone on the client side to sign off.

Approval bottlenecks are one of the most common causes of blown deadlines and compressed timelines in agency work. The frustrating part is that they are almost always predictable. The same projects, the same clients, the same delay patterns. Yet most agencies still treat approval delays as a client problem rather than a process problem they can solve.

This piece is about why that shift in perspective matters and what you can do to build an approval process that does not rely on chasing people.

## Why Approvals Stall (And Why It Is Rarely the Client's Fault)

When an approval delays, the instinct is to blame the client for being slow or disorganised. Sometimes that is true. But more often, the stall happens because the client does not have a clear process for giving approval on their end.

Think about what you are typically asking a client to do. Review a piece of work, collect feedback from colleagues who may or may not be cc'd, consolidate that feedback into a usable response, and then send it back to you. That is four distinct steps, none of which you have given them a structure for.

When you send work without a review process attached to it, you are leaving the client to invent their own. Some are good at this. Many are not. And when the process breaks down on their side, you are the one waiting.

The solution is to take the process off their plate. You decide how approvals work. You provide the tool. You make it as easy as possible for the right people to give feedback in the right place.

## The Two Types of Approval Bottleneck

It helps to understand which kind of bottleneck you are dealing with, because the fix is slightly different for each.

The first type is a stakeholder access problem. The people who need to approve the work cannot easily see or interact with it. They are getting forwarded emails with attachments, or links to shared drives they do not have access to, or PDFs that were not properly labelled. They want to give feedback but the friction is too high.

The second type is a decision-making problem. The people who need to approve the work are not sure they have the authority to do so, or they are waiting for someone more senior to weigh in first. The feedback loop breaks because nobody knows whose job it is to say yes.

Both types require you to get upstream in the client's process. For the first, the fix is a dedicated review environment: one link, clear instructions, feedback in context. For the second, the fix is agreeing who has approval authority before the work is shared. A simple question at project kickoff, Who is the final sign-off on creative, can save you a week of waiting later.

## Build the Approval Step Into Your Process Documents

The biggest lever agencies have is documentation. Your project process should include an approval stage with explicit steps, timelines, and named contacts.

When you send work for review, do not just send the work. Send a short covering note that tells the client exactly what you need from them, who should be involved, how to submit feedback, and by when. Something like: This is ready for your team's review. All notes should come through the review link below. We need consolidated feedback by Thursday so we can hit the Friday delivery date.

This does the work of managing the approval process on your behalf. The client knows what is expected. The deadline is visible. The method is clear.

When this is part of every project, clients start to internalise it. After a few projects with you, they know how approvals work. They brief their stakeholders before you send the review. They consolidate internally before responding. The bottleneck does not disappear entirely, but it becomes much less frequent.

## What to Do When Approvals Still Stall

Even with good process, approvals sometimes stall. A stakeholder goes on holiday. An internal decision drags on. The person who was supposed to review it forgot.

When this happens, escalate quickly and clearly. A brief, professional message that references the agreed timeline is usually enough: Just checking in on the review, we need feedback by end of day to stay on track for the Friday delivery. You are not nagging. You are holding the client to what was agreed.

If a delay looks like it will push the delivery date, communicate that proactively rather than hoping it will resolve itself. Clients generally respond better to early warnings than to last-minute notifications.

For serious delays, more than a few days beyond the agreed review window, it is worth having a conversation about whether the scope or timeline needs to be adjusted. This is easier to do early than late, and it protects you from being held responsible for a deadline that the client's approval process caused you to miss.

## Centralising Review to Eliminate Feedback Scatter

One of the most practical things agencies can do is move all feedback out of email and into a dedicated review environment. Not because email is always bad, but because it creates scattered, unstructured feedback that is hard to act on and impossible to track.

When feedback arrives in a central tool, alongside the actual work, a few things happen. Stakeholders see each other's notes before they add their own, which reduces duplicates. Context is preserved. And you have a record of what was said and what was resolved, which matters when a client disputes a decision six weeks later.

The agencies that move fastest are not necessarily the ones with the best clients. They are the ones who have built a process that makes it easy for clients to keep up. Fixing your approval bottleneck is one of the highest-return process improvements an agency can make.
