You quoted three rounds of revisions. Eight emails later, you're on round six and the deadline is tomorrow.

Every freelancer has been there. You scope the project carefully, agree on a price, and start work feeling confident. Then the feedback starts arriving. One comment in an email. A voice note from the client. A PDF with sticky notes. A WhatsApp message with "oh, one more thing."

Before you know it, you're doing twice the work for the same fee.

Going over budget isn't usually about bad planning or undercharging. It's about what happens when feedback has no structure.

---

## The Feedback Problem Nobody Talks About

Most budget overruns don't start with a client asking for the moon. They start with small, easy-to-miss things. A request slipped into a casual email. An unclear approval. A vague "looks great, just tweak a few bits" with no specifics attached.

The problem isn't the feedback itself. It's where it lives.

When feedback comes in from five different places, you lose track of what's been agreed, what's been actioned, and what the client actually signed off on. That confusion costs you time, and time is what you're billing.

Even well-intentioned clients cause scope creep when they have no clear channel for sharing feedback. Without a defined process, they fill the gaps with whatever feels easiest. That usually means an email here, a comment there, and a late-night message that kicks off another round of changes you didn't account for.

---

## How Revision Rounds Quietly Eat Your Profit

Think about your last project. How many rounds of revisions did you quote? Two? Three?

Now count how many actually happened.

Every extra round takes time you didn't price for. Even if each one feels small, the hours add up fast. Two hours here. Ninety minutes there. A late evening fixing something you thought was finished last week.

That's not a client problem. That's a process problem.

When there's no clear record of what feedback was given, nothing stops a client from adding to it. Or repeating it. Or changing their mind about something they already approved. Without a paper trail, you're working on memory, and so are they.

The result is a project that quietly doubles in scope while the invoice stays the same.

---

## How to Stop It Before It Starts

The fix isn't to work faster or charge more (though reviewing your rates never hurts). It's to make the feedback process clear from day one.

Here's what actually works.

**Give clients one place to leave feedback.** Not email, not WhatsApp, not both. One place. This removes the confusion of trying to consolidate notes from multiple sources, and it means you have a clear record of exactly what was requested and when.

**Set a round limit and make it visible.** Put it in your contract and remind clients of it when feedback is due. "This is round one of two" is a simple sentence that keeps scope in check. It also signals professionalism, and most clients respond well to that.

**Get written sign-off before moving to the next stage.** A quick "Happy for me to proceed?" email works perfectly. Verbal approvals don't hold up when a client later says "I didn't know that was final." Having confirmation in writing protects you both.

**Brief clients on the feedback process at the start.** Not in a heavy-handed way, just a short explanation of how it works: where to leave comments, when to expect to review, and what happens after final sign-off. Clients who understand the process cause far fewer problems mid-project.

These aren't aggressive tactics. They're professional standards that make the whole experience smoother for everyone.

---

## What to Do When It's Already Gone Sideways

Sometimes you're already in it. The project has run long, the client keeps adding requests, and you're not sure how to raise the issue without damaging the relationship.

Start with a clear, calm summary of where things stand. Something like: "We're now on round four. The original quote covered three rounds, so any additional revisions from here will be billed at my hourly rate of [X]. Happy to carry on, just wanted to keep you in the loop."

Keep the tone matter-of-fact, not apologetic. You haven't done anything wrong. You're honouring the agreement you both signed up to.

Most clients respond well to this when it's handled without drama. They often don't realise how many rounds have passed. A factual update is far better received than a tense conversation at the end of the project.

And the clients who push back? That's useful information too. A client who refuses to respect a clearly agreed scope is a client worth re-evaluating before your next project together.

The earlier you address scope creep, the easier it is to resolve. Leaving it until invoice time creates tension that could have been avoided with one short email in the middle of the project.

---

## The Bigger Picture

Budget overruns are frustrating, but they're rarely inevitable. The projects that stay on track are the ones where feedback is centralised, expectations are set early, and scope changes are addressed calmly and professionally.

When your feedback process is clear, your clients feel confident. They know where to go, what to expect, and when decisions are final. That clarity reduces back-and-forth, limits revision rounds, and keeps your time protected.
