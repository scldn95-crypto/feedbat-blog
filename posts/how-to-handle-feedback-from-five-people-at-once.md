# How to Handle Feedback From Five People at Once

You send the edit. Then the replies start rolling in. The brand manager wants the logo bigger. The marketing lead wants the music changed. The founder thinks the pacing is off. The social media manager has a different cut in mind. And then there's the client's assistant, who's just forwarded notes from someone you've never even spoken to.

Five people. Five sets of opinions. And somehow, you're supposed to make one video that satisfies all of them.

Multi-stakeholder feedback is one of the hardest parts of being a creator. It's not just the volume of notes — it's the contradiction, the lack of clarity, and the feeling that no matter what you do, someone's going to be unhappy. But it doesn't have to be this way. With the right approach, you can take five voices and turn them into one clear direction.

## Why Multi-Stakeholder Feedback Gets So Messy

The problem isn't the number of people — it's the lack of structure around how they give feedback.

When everyone has their own way of commenting (emails, voice notes, DMs, comment threads, calls), you end up with notes scattered across five different places. You miss things. You act on outdated notes. You make a change for one person that breaks something another person approved.

There's also the hierarchy problem. Not all feedback is equal, but without a clear process, it's hard to know whose opinion takes priority. You end up either trying to satisfy everyone (impossible) or making your best guess about what matters most (risky).

The solution is to stop treating feedback as a conversation and start treating it as a process.

## Set Up a Single Source of Truth Before You Share

Before you send anything for review, decide where feedback will live. One place. Not email, not Slack, not a shared Google Doc alongside a voice note — one dedicated space where every stakeholder leaves their notes.

This is the single most important change you can make. When all feedback comes in through the same channel, you can see conflicts immediately, spot patterns, and respond to everything in one go rather than chasing notes across five apps.

Tools like FeedBat are built specifically for this. You share a link, stakeholders watch and leave time-stamped comments directly on the video, and you see everything in one place. No more digging through inboxes to piece together what everyone said.

Get stakeholders to agree to this process upfront. Frame it as a way to make their lives easier, not just yours. When they see how much cleaner the communication becomes, they'll appreciate it.

## Define Who Has the Final Say

Before the review process starts, establish a decision-maker. This is the person whose feedback takes priority when notes conflict. It might be the brand lead, the founder, the commissioning editor — whoever is ultimately responsible for the output.

Make this explicit. You can do it gently: "Just so I know how to handle any conflicting notes — who should I treat as the final decision-maker on this project?"

Most stakeholders will appreciate the clarity. And it gives you a professional way to navigate conflict when it arises. Instead of getting caught between two opposing opinions, you can go back to the decision-maker and say: "I've got two different directions from the team — here are both options. Which do you want me to run with?"

This isn't about dismissing anyone's input. It's about having a clear path forward when the voices don't align.

## Batch and Categorise the Notes

Once feedback comes in, don't start making changes immediately. First, read through everything and group the notes by type.

There are usually three categories:

**Must-fix:** Factual errors, brand guideline breaches, legal issues, technical problems. These get done, no debate.

**Should-fix:** Legitimate creative improvements that don't contradict other feedback. These are worth actioning if they improve the work.

**Consider:** Personal preferences, minor stylistic tweaks, changes that conflict with something another stakeholder approved. These need to be filtered through the decision-maker before you touch them.

This approach stops you from knee-jerk reacting to every note. It also gives you a clear conversation starter when you need to push back: "I've reviewed all the notes — here are the ones I've actioned, and here are a couple that conflict with each other. Can we get a quick call to align?"

That's a professional, organised response. It demonstrates control over the process rather than confusion.

## Communicate Progress Clearly

One reason feedback rounds drag on is that stakeholders feel out of the loop. They send notes, hear nothing, and assume their feedback is being ignored. So they send more notes. Or they start chasing via email and calls, pulling you away from the actual work.

Fix this with a simple update after each round. When you receive feedback, acknowledge it. When you start working on revisions, let the group know. When you're ready for the next round of review, share it with a clear summary of what changed.

This doesn't need to be long. A short message saying "Got all the notes — I'll have the revised cut ready by Thursday" does the job. It reassures people that the process is moving and stops the follow-up messages before they start.

FeedBat makes this easier by keeping all communication in one thread tied to the actual content. Everyone can see the notes, the replies, and the status without needing a separate email chain.

## Know When to Push Back

Not all feedback deserves equal weight, and not every note makes the work better. Part of handling multi-stakeholder feedback well is knowing when to push back, and how to do it without causing friction.

The key is to separate the feedback from the person. You're not rejecting their opinion — you're explaining why a particular change would harm the output. "I understand the instinct to make the logo bigger, but at that size it starts to distract from the message we're trying to land in the first ten seconds" is a professional, reasoned response. It invites dialogue rather than closing it down.

Pick your battles. Save pushback for the notes that would genuinely compromise the work. For everything else, trust the process, action what makes sense, and escalate genuine conflicts to the decision-maker.

## Bring It All Together

Handling feedback from five people at once is a skill. It takes a clear process, a single place for notes, a defined decision-maker, and consistent communication.

The creators who manage it best aren't the ones who try to please everyone — they're the ones who build a structure that makes the whole review process smoother for everyone involved.

When five people have opinions, the answer isn't to juggle five conversations. It's to create one process that works for all of them.
